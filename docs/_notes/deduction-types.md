
While TurboTax makes finding them easier, it doesn't create its own deductions — it simply walks you through the U.S. tax code. Because the IRS offers hundreds of specific write-offs, listing absolutely *all* of them is nearly impossible.
However, the tax code groups them into a few major categories. Here is a comprehensive breakdown of the deductions you can claim.
## 1. The Standard Deduction
Most taxpayers take the standard deduction rather than itemizing because it shelters a large portion of income without requiring you to track individual receipts.
For the **2025 tax year**, the standard deduction amounts are:
| Filing Status | 2025 Deduction |
|---|---|
| **Single / Married Filing Separately** | $15,750 |
| **Head of Household** | $23,625 |
| **Married Filing Jointly** | $31,500 |
## 2. "Above-the-Line" Deductions 
Also known as adjustments to income, you can claim these *even if* you take the standard deduction. They lower your Adjusted Gross Income (AGI).
 * **Student loan interest:** Up to $2,500 of interest paid on qualified student loans.
 * **Traditional IRA contributions:** Deductible up to the annual contribution limit, depending on your income and workplace retirement plans.
 * **Health Savings Account (HSA) contributions:** Fully deductible up to the annual limit if you have a high-deductible health plan.
 * **Educator expenses:** Up to $300 for out-of-pocket classroom supplies (increases to $350 in 2026).
 * **Alimony payments:** Only for divorce or separation agreements executed before 2019.
 * **Moving expenses:** Restricted exclusively to active-duty military members moving under orders.
## 3. Itemized Deductions (Schedule A)
If your deductible expenses add up to more than your standard deduction, you should itemize. You cannot take both the standard deduction and itemized deductions.
 * **State and Local Taxes (SALT):** You can deduct property taxes plus either state income tax *or* state sales tax. For 2025, the cap on this deduction increased significantly to $40,000 for most filers.
 * **Mortgage interest:** Interest paid on the first $750,000 of mortgage debt for a primary or secondary home.
 * **Medical and dental expenses:** You can deduct unreimbursed medical costs, but only the amount that exceeds 7.5% of your AGI.
 * **Charitable donations:** Cash, property, or mileage driven for registered 501(c)(3) charities.
 * **Disaster losses:** Losses to personal property from federally declared natural disasters.
## 4. Self-Employed and Business Deductions (Schedule C)
If you own a business, freelance, or do gig work, you can deduct expenses that are "ordinary and necessary" for your trade. These are separate from your personal deductions.
 * **Qualified Business Income (QBI):** Allows eligible self-employed individuals to deduct up to 20% of their net business income.
 * **Home office:** A percentage of your rent/mortgage, utilities, and internet based on the square footage used exclusively for business.
 * **Vehicle expenses:** Tracked via the standard mileage rate or actual expenses (gas, maintenance, insurance).
 * **Self-employed health insurance:** Premiums paid for medical, dental, and long-term care for yourself and dependents.
 * **Start-up costs:** Up to $5,000 in your first year of business.
 * **Operating expenses:** Advertising, software subscriptions, office supplies, travel, legal fees, and contract labor.
 * **Retirement contributions:** Contributions to a SEP IRA, SIMPLE IRA, or Solo 401(k).
## 5. New Temporary Deductions (2025–2028)
Recent legislation introduced two new temporary deductions that apply to specific types of workers:
 * **Overtime pay deduction:** Eligible workers can deduct up to $12,500 of overtime pay ($25,000 for married filing jointly). This only applies to the "half" portion of time-and-a-half pay.
 * **Tip income deduction:** Workers in traditionally tipped industries can deduct up to $25,000 in tip income.
